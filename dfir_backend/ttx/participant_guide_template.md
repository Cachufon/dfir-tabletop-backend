## 1) Exercise overview

You are participating in a **discussion-based cybersecurity tabletop exercise (TTX)**.
The purpose is to improve readiness by testing:
- decision-making under time pressure,
- escalation and authority clarity,
- communications governance (internal and external),
- legal/regulatory awareness (as applicable),
- and identification of actionable improvements.

**This is a no-fault learning exercise.** It is not an audit and does not prove technical control effectiveness.
No systems will be accessed or tested during the session.

## 4) Rules of engagement (read this)

1. **No-fault learning environment:** The goal is improvement, not grading individuals.
2. **Discussion-based:** No systems are touched; we focus on decisions, communications, and process.
3. **State assumptions:** If information is missing, clearly state what you assume and what you would request.
4. **Time-boxed decisions:** The facilitator may force a decision to keep the exercise moving.
5. **One conversation at a time:** Avoid side threads; use a “parking lot” for later discussion.
6. **Confidentiality:** Treat discussion and outputs as sensitive unless agreed otherwise.

---

## 5) What to bring (mental model)

Be ready to answer at a high level:
- What decisions your role owns during an incident
- Who you escalate to (and when)
- What information you would request to make key decisions
- How you would communicate (internally/external) and who approves messaging
- What tradeoffs matter (risk vs business impact)

## 7) Session structure (typical 90 minutes)

- Opening & rules (5 minutes)
- Scenario context (5 minutes)
- Inject cycles (60–70 minutes)
  - Each inject introduces new information and forces decisions
- Hotwash (10–15 minutes)
  - What worked, what slowed down, what was unclear, top improvements

---

## 8) Expectations during injects

When you respond to an inject, aim to include:
- The decision you would make now (and why)
- Who owns that decision (role)
- The immediate next 30 minutes of actions (role-owned)
- What information you need (and who requests it)
- Communications: who needs to know, what to say, and who approves
